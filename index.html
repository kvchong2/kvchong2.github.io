Leading Causes of Death in the United States, 1999-2017

The National Center for Health Statistics released a data set based on the death certificates of individuals in the United States and the District of Columbia (Washington D.C.). A data normalization against the population was made and provided as an "age-adjusted death rate (per 100,000 population). It is based on the U.S. census data that takes place every decade (10 years).

The following charts examine this data by looking at the rates for the causes of deaths relative to each other across the years that are available in the data.

Scroll up or down, use the up/down keys, or click on the pagination dots on the right edge of the scene to navigate.

The rates are based on the 2000 U.S. standard population. Population before 2010 on non-census years are based on updated intercensal population estimates.

The population from 2011-2017 are based on the 2010 census estimate as of July 1, 2010.

The causes of death are classified by the International Classification of Diseases, Tenth Revision(ICD-10). Cause-of-death statistics are based on the underlying cause of death.

The source of this data set is from https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/data-visualization/mortality-leading-causes/index.htm; NCHS, National Vital Statistics System, mortality data (see http://www.cdc.gov/nchs/deaths.htm); and CDC WONDER (see http://wonder.cdc.gov).

U.S. Death Trends for 10 Top Causes

Since 1999, there has been an overall decrease in age-adjusted death rates across the United States for all causes of death.

Decreasing Death Trends

The leading causes of death in the United States have consistently been heart disease and cancer. Data indicates that death rates from both of these causes have been declining, significantly contributing to the overall decline in age-adjusted death rates across the country.

Cardiovascular Disease Trends

Although the death rates have shown the most decline for heart disease, the death rates for related cardiovascular-related diseases such as stroke and diabetes, have also been decreasing, albeit at a lower pace.

Increasing Death Trends

However, three causes of death have shown a rise in age-adjusted death rates in the United States. This includes unintentional injuries, Alzheimer's disease, and suicide.

State Trends Comparison

Between 1999 and 2017, data on deaths for each state reveals notable disparities in age-adjusted death rates per 100,000 population by 2017. States like Mississippi, West Virginia, Alabama, Kentucky, and Oklahoma reported higher death rates compared to states such as Hawaii, California, New York, Connecticut, and Minnesota.
